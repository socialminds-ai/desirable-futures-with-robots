IThe Urgency

Robots are at our door.

Within one to two years, fully autonomous robots will be present in everyday life — at home, at work, in care, in public space. The pace is unlike anything human–robot interaction has prepared for.

Three years after the introduction of ChatGPT, we already see the cost of letting a single narrative settle uncontested. With embodied agents, the stakes multiply: robots will not compete with humans on one axis, but on many at once.

The window in which we can shape the narrative is closing. It is closing this year, and next.

IIThe Dominant Narrative

Efficiency. Productivity. Replacement.

Robots, we are told, will improve human efficiency. They will take the dull, the dirty, the dangerous. They will save us time. They will do more, for less.

Embedded in this story, inseparable from it, is the idea of competition with humans.

It is the same story we are already living with AI agents. With embodied, autonomous robots, it will extend into every domain of life: domestic, relational, civic, intimate.

IIIThe Proposition

Another narrative is possible. And desirable.

Not AI to augment humans. Not robots as obedient servitors with superhuman powers.
But AI as an alternative form of cognition, alongside human — different, partial, situated, sometimes useful, sometimes other.

IVThe Series

One hundred workshops. Distributed.

100

workshops in total, staged ten by ten so the format refines itself iteratively before scaling.

∞

publics: children, adolescents, older adults, warehouse workers, care workers, students, families — across cultures and socio-economic contexts.

1

shared reporting format, so results compare, aggregate, and accumulate into something larger than any single workshop.

Workshops are led by local facilitators, primarily from the HRI research community. Protocols can vary by audience — role-play, drawing, brainstorming, interviews — but every workshop reports back in the same form, so the collective picture remains legible.

These workshops do not replace long-term in-situ deployment. They do something different, and necessary: they plant alternative seeds, in many soils, before the dominant narrative finishes setting.

VWhat If

We work from inversion.

Each workshop opens with a what if that turns the dominant story on its head — and asks participants to imagine a desirable future growing from there. Not dystopia. Not a clever subversion. A future they would actually want.

  1. 01

    What if robots didn't always say yes to our orders?

  2. 02

    What if a robot was clumsy, and needed our help — all the time?

  3. 03

    What if a robot didn't try to save your time, but to waste it well?

  4. 04

    What if a robot brought you something you never asked for?

  5. +

    A growing bank, contributed by facilitators and participants. Have one? Send it →

VIThe Approach

Start from the future, not the robot.

01

What future do I want?

Begin from a life, a longing, a fear. "The worst thing for me would be…"

02

Where might a robot fit?

If at all. Not as solution. As participant in a picture already drawn.

03

What would such a robot be?

Its role, its shape, its place, its refusals. The thing it does not do.

Not this:

1. I have a robot.
2. What can I do with it?

We resist techno-solutionism — and we keep room for futures only possible with robots. Both at once.

VIIWhat Facilitators Receive

A shared kit. Light, adaptable, rigorous.

  • One-page rationale

    The series' premise, written for adaptation. Translations for children, adolescents, warehouse workers, older adults.

  • The what if bank

    A growing set of inversion prompts, contributed by the network, free to use and remix.

  • Shared reporting form

    Title · short description · image · structured answers. So results aggregate into something larger.

  • Example formats

    Role-play (recommended — it places people in situation), drawing, brainstorming, structured interviews.

VIIIThe Horizon

April 2027 — and ten years beyond.

April 2027

Final event

Results from the series presented to a broad audience — researchers, policy makers, industry, media. A public synthesis of what 100 workshops, distributed across the world, have imagined together.

The series plants seeds.

· For people to feel safe today, in the face of robots arriving.

· For designing desirable futures with robots, on a ten-year horizon.

· For challenging the view of robots as obedient servitors with superhuman powers — and building alternatives in its place.
